I-V Relations & Current Gain

I-V

$$ \begin{gather} \begin{aligned} I_C &= A_E q \frac{ D_B n_{iB}^2 }{W_B N_B}(e^{qV_{BE}/kT} - 1) \\ I_B &= A_E q \frac{ D_E n_{iE}^2 }{W_E N_E}(e^{qV_{BE}/kT} - 1) \end{aligned} \end{gather} $$

Current Gain

$$ \begin{gather} \begin{aligned} \beta_F &= \frac{ I_C }{ I_B } = \frac{ D_B n_{iB}^2 W_E N_E }{ D_E n_{iE}^2 W_B N_B} \\ \alpha_F &= \frac{ I_C }{ I_E } \\ &= \frac{ \beta_F }{ 1 + \beta_F } \end{aligned} \end{gather} $$

Base-With Modulation

$$ \begin{equation} r_o = \Big( \frac{ \partial I_C }{ \partial V_{CE} } \Big)^{-1} = \frac{ V_A }{ I_C } \end{equation} $$

  • Large $V_C(r_o)$ is desirable for a Large Voltage Gain

Emitter-to-Collector Transit Time and Kirk Effect

  1. Reducing Transit Time

To reduce the total transit time, emitter and depletion layers must be thin, too.

  1. Kirk Effect

Kirk effect or base widening: At high IC the base widens into the collector. Wider base means larger tF .
